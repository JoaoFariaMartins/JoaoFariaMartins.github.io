  • Exercise 14. (*) Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be topological spaces. Suppose that \(f\colon X \to Y\) is continuous. Let \(x,x'\in X\). Let \(y=f(x)\) and \(y'=f(x')\).
    Let \(\phi ,\gamma \colon [0,1]\to X\) be paths in \(X\), with \(\gamma (0)=x=\phi (0)\) and \(\gamma (1)=x'=\phi (1)\). Suppose that \(\phi \) and \(\gamma \) are path-homotopic. Prove that \(f\circ \gamma , f\circ \phi \colon [0,1]\to Y\) are path-homotopic paths, both starting in \(y\) and both ending in \(y'\).
    Hint: Adapt the comments just after Definition 5.41 of the notes.

    Solution: First of all \((f\circ \gamma )(0)=(f\circ \phi )(0)=f(x)=y\) and \((f\circ \gamma )(1)=(f\circ \phi )(1)=f(x')=y'\). So \(f\circ \gamma , f\circ \phi \colon [0,1]\to Y\) each starts in \(y\), and each ends in \(y'\), so it makes sense to address whether the paths \(f\circ \gamma \) and \(f\circ \phi \) are path-homotopic.

    Let \(H\colon [0,1]^2 \to X\) be a path-homotopy between \(\phi \) and \(\gamma \). So \(H\colon [0,1]^2 \to X\) is continuous, and satisfies:

    • • \(H(t,0)=\phi (t)\) and \(H(t,1)=\gamma (t)\), for each \(t \in [0,1]\);

    • • \(H(0,s)=x\) and \(H(1,s)=x'\), for each \(s \in [0,1]\).

    Take \(J=f\circ H\), so \(J\) is a continuous function, \([0,1]^2 \to Y\), since it is the composite of continuous functions. Moreover:

    • • \(J(t,0)=f(H(t,0))=(f\circ \phi )(t)\), and \(J(t,1)=f(H(t,1))=(f\circ \gamma )(t)\), for each \(t \in [0,1]\);

    • • \(J(0,s)=f(H(0,s))=f(x)=y\), and \(J(1,s)=f(H(1,s))=f(x')=y'\), for each \(s \in [0,1]\).

     

    ∎

  • Exercise 15. Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be topological spaces, let \(x \in X\) and let \(h\colon X\rightarrow Y\) be a homeomorphism. Prove that for any \(x\in X\), the induced map \(h_*\colon \pi _1(X,x)\rightarrow \pi _1(Y,h(x))\) is a group isomorphism (i.e. a bijective homomorphism).

Solution: Given that \(h\colon X \to Y\) is continuous, we already know that we have an induced homomorphism \(h_*\colon \pi _1(X,x)\rightarrow \pi _1(Y,h(x))\), of groups. So it suffices to prove that \(h_*\colon \pi _1(X,x)\rightarrow \pi _1(Y,h(x))\) is bijective. We give two different arguments:

  • First proof:
    Let us show that \(h_*\) is bijective. To see that it is surjective, note that, for every loop \(\gamma \) in \(Y\) with base \(h(x)\), the path \(h^{-1} \circ \gamma \) is a loop in \(X\) with base \(x\) and furthermore \(h_*([h^{-1} \circ \gamma ]) = [h \circ h^{-1} \circ \gamma ] = [\gamma ]\). Hence \(h_*\) is surjective.

    To see that it is injective, let us fix \(\gamma _1\) and \(\gamma _2\) to be two loops in \(X\) with base \(x\) satisfying that \(h_*([\gamma _1])=h_*([\gamma _2])\). We show that \([\gamma _1] = [\gamma _2]\).

    Recall that \(h_*([\gamma _1])= [h\circ \gamma _1]\) and \(h_*([\gamma _2])= [h\circ \gamma _2]\). So \([h\circ \gamma _1] = [h\circ \gamma _2]\). Now recall that, since \(h\) is a homeomorphism, \(h^{-1}\) is continuous. So we have that \((h^{-1})_*:\pi _1(Y,h(x)) \rightarrow \pi _1(X,x)\) is a well-defined function that is a group homomorphism. In particular \((h^{-1})_*([h\circ \gamma _1]) = (h^{-1})_*([h\circ \gamma _2])\). Finally note that

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{}{3}\)

    \begin{align*} (h^{-1})_*([h\circ \gamma _1]) &= [h^{-1}\circ h \circ \gamma _1] = [\gamma _1], \\ (h^{-1})_*([h\circ \gamma _2]) &= [h^{-1}\circ h \circ \gamma _2] = [\gamma _2]. \end{align*} We conclude that \([\gamma _1] = [\gamma _2]\), as desired.

  • Second proof:
    It suffices to find an explicit inverse of \(h_*\colon \pi _1(X,x)\rightarrow \pi _1(Y,h(x))\). Take \(g\colon Y \to X\) to be the inverse of \(f\), also a continuous function, because \(h\colon X \to Y\) is a homeomorphism. By definition of inverse function, \(h\circ g=\id _Y\) and \(g\circ h=\id _X\). Take \(g_*\colon \pi _1(Y,h(x))\to \pi _1(X,x)\). We claim that \(g_*\) is the inverse of \(h_*\). So note:

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{}{3}\)

    \begin{align*} g_*\circ h_*&=(g\circ h)_*, \textrm { by Theorem 5.44.}\\ &=(\id _X)_*\\ &=\id _{\pi _1(X,x)}, \textrm { again by Theorem 5.44.}\\ \end{align*} Analogously,

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{}{3}\)

    \begin{align*} h_*\circ g_*&=(h\circ g)_*, \textrm { by Theorem 5.44.}\\ &=(\id _Y)_*\\ &=\id _{\pi _1(Y,f(x))}, \textrm { again by Theorem 5.44.}\\ \end{align*}

 

∎

We now introduce a new definition, which is also in the lecture notes: See 5.12 Strong deformation retracts.

Definition: Let \(X\) be a topological space. A subset \(K\subseteq X\) is called a strong deformation retract of \(X\), if there exists a continuous function, \(H\colon X \times [0,1] \to X\) such that:

  • 1. \(H(x,0)=x\), for all \(x \in X\),

  • 2. \(H(x,1)\in K\), for all \(x \in X\),

  • 3. \(H(k,t)=k\), for all \(k \in K\) and \(t\in [0,1]\).

We call the map \(H\colon X\times [0,1]\to X\) a strong deformation retraction, from \(X\) to \(K\).

  • Exercise 16. Prove that \(\R \times \{0\}\) is a strong deformation retract of \(\R \times [-1,1]\), where we consider the topologies induced by the standard topology of \(\R ^2\).
    Hint: You can use the fact that the product topology in \((\R \times [-1,1])\times [0,1]\) is the topology that \(\R ^3\), with the Euclidean topology, induces on \(\R \times [-1,1]\times [0,1]\).

    Solution (given): Recall that

    \[\R \times [-1,1]=\{(x,y): x \in \R \textrm { and } y \in [-1,1] \}.\]

    We consider the function:

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{}{3}\)

    \begin{align*} H\colon (\R \times [-1,1]) \times [0,1]&\to \R \times [-1,1]\\ (x,y,t)&\mapsto \big ( x, y(1-t) \big ). \end{align*} This function is continuous. Indeed letting \(\pi _1\colon \R \times [-1,1]\to \R \) and \(\pi _2\colon \R \times [-1,1]\to [-1,1]\) be the projections onto the first and second variables, we have that \((\pi _1\circ H) (x,y,t)=x\) and \((\pi _2\circ H )(x,y,t)=y(1-t)\). These are continuous functions, \(\R \times [-1,1]\times [0,1]\to \R \) and \(\R \times [-1,1]\times [0,1]\to [-1,1]\), since they are polynomials, in three variables.

    Finally we clearly have:

    • • \(H(x,y,0)=(x,y)\), for all \((x,y) \in \R \times [-1,1]\).

    • • \(H(x,y,1)=(x,0) \in \R \times \{0\}\) for all \((x,y) \in \R \times [-1,1]\).

    • • \(H(x,y, t)=(x,y)\), for all \((x,y) \in \R \times \{0\}\) and \(t\in [0,1]\). This is because \(H(x,0,t)=(x,0)\).

     

    ∎

Yet, we now need another definition. Let \(X\) be a topological space.

Definition: We say that \(K\subseteq X\) is a retract of \(X\) if there exists a continuous function \(p\colon X \to K\) such that \(p(k)=k\), for all \(k \in K\). (Here \(K\) is given the induced topology.)

  • Exercise 17. Prove that if \(K\) is a strong deformation retract of \(X\) then \(K\) is a retract of \(X\).

    Solution: Let \(H\colon X\times [0,1]\to X\) be a strong deformation retract of \(X\) onto \(K\). We consider the function \(f\colon X \to X\) such that \(f(x)=H(x,1)\). Given that, in particular

    • 1. \(H(k,t)=k\), for all \(k \in K\),

    • 2. \(H(x,1)\in K\), for all \(x \in X\).

    we have that \(f(k)=k\), for all \(k \in K\) and also that \(f(x)\in K\) for all \(x \in X\).

    We claim that \(f\colon X \to X\) is continuous. The properties of the product topology give that the function \(\iota _1\colon X \to X\times [0,1]\) such that \(\iota _1(x)=(x,1)\) is continuous. Indeed this follows from the fact that the two component functions, \(X \to X\), with \(x\mapsto x\), and \(X \to [0,1]\), \(x \mapsto 1\) are continuous, because the first is the identity function, \(X \to X\), and the second a constant function.

    So \(f=H\circ \iota _1\) is a continuous function \(X\to X\). Finally, since \(f(X)\subseteq K\), the co-restriction \(f^K\colon X \to K\), with \(x\mapsto f(x)\) ,is continuous, by the properties of the induced topology.  

    ∎

  • Exercise 18. Let \(K\) be a strong deformation retract of \(X\). Prove that \(K\) is path-connected if, and only if, \(X\) is path connected.

    Solution (given): \(\Leftarrow \)
    Suppose \(X\) is path-connected. Let us prove that \(K\) is path-connected. Let \(x, y \in K\). Let us find a path \(\gamma \colon [0,1] \to K\), with \(\gamma (0)=x\) and \(\gamma (1)=y\). Let \(\phi \colon [0,1] \to X\) be a path, starting in \(x\) and ending in \(y\), which exists, since \(X\) is path-connected. We know that \(K\) is a strong deformation retract of \(X\), and hence a retract of \(X\). So, choose \(p\colon X \to K\) with \(p(k)=k\), for all \(k \in K\). The function \(\gamma =p\circ \phi \) is path in \(K\), and moreover, \(\gamma (0)=x\) and \(\gamma (1)=y\).
    NB: Alternatively, we can apply the previous exercise together with exercise 2.

    \(\implies \)
    Suppose that \(K\) is path-connected. Let us prove that \(X\) is path-connected. We choose a strong deformation retraction, \(H\colon X\times [0,1]\to X\), from \(X\) to \(K\).

    Let \(x,y \in X\). Let us build a path \(\gamma \) connecting \(x\) to \(y\). Consider the following three paths:

    • • \(\gamma _1\colon [0,1]\to X\) such that \(\gamma _1(t)=H(x,t)\). So \(\gamma _1(0)=x\) and \(\gamma _1(1)=H(x,1)\in K\).

    • • \(\gamma _2\colon [0,1]\to X\) such that \(\gamma _2(t)=H(y,t)\). So \(\gamma _2(0)=y\) and \(\gamma _2(1)=H(y,1)\in K\).

    • • Consider a path \(\phi \colon [0,1]\to K\), connecting \(\gamma _1(1)\) and \(\gamma _2(1)\), which exists since \(K\) is path-connected.

    A path connecting \(x\) to \(y\) is then, e.g., the concatenation \((\gamma _1 * \phi ) *\overline {\gamma _2}\).  

    ∎

  • Exercise 19. (*) Suppose that \(K\) is a strong deformation retract of \(X\). Let \(k\in K\). Prove that if \(\pi _1(K,k)\) is trivial then so is \(\pi _1(X,k)\).
    Hint: Use the fact that the relation of path-homotopy between paths is an equivalence relation. In particular, if \(\gamma \sim \gamma '\) and if \(\gamma '\sim \gamma ''\), then \(\gamma \sim \gamma ''\).

    Solution: It suffices to prove that \(\pi _1(X,k)\) has only one element. To this end we prove that any path \(\gamma \colon [0,1]\to X\), starting and ending in \(k\) is homotopic to \(e_k\), the constant path at \(k\). Consider a strong deformation retration \(H\colon X\times [0,1]\to X\) of \(X\) onto \(K\). Consider the function:

    \(\seteqnumber{0}{}{3}\)

    \begin{align*} H_1\colon [0,1]^2 &\to X\\ (s,t)&\mapsto H(\gamma (s),t). \end{align*} Claim: \(H_1\) is continuous.

    Proof of the claim
    We first prove that the function \(F\colon [0,1]^2 \to X\times [0,1]\) such that \(F(s,t)=(\gamma (s),t)\) is continuous. The properties of the product topology tell us that it suffices proving that \(F_1\colon [0,1]^2 \to X\) such that \(F_1(s,t)=\gamma (s)\) and \(F_2\colon [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]\) such that \(F_2(s,t)=t\) are continuous. The function \(F_2\) is continuous as it is the projection \([0,1]\times [0,1]\to [0,1]\), on the second variable. The function \(F_1\) is continuous since it is the composition with the projection \([0,1]\times [0,1]\to [0,1]\), on the first variable with \(\gamma \colon [0,1]\to X\).

    Now observe that \(H_1=H\circ F\).

    Now note that \(H_1\) is a path homotopy, starting at \(\gamma \). Indeed, \(H_1(s,0)=H(\gamma (s),0)=\gamma (s)\) for every \(s\in [0,1]\). Moreover \(H_1(0,t)=H(\gamma (0),t)=H(k,t)=k\), and \(H_1(1,t)=H(\gamma (1),t)=H(k,t)=k\). Note that \(H_1\) connects \(\gamma \) with the path \(\phi \colon [0,1]\to K\) such that \(\phi (s)=H_1(s,1)=H(\gamma (s),1)\). This is a path in \(K\), starting and ending in \(k\). And since \(\pi _1(K,k)\) is trivial, then \(\phi \sim e_k\). Since \(\gamma \sim \phi \) we have that \(\gamma \sim e_k\).  

    ∎

  • Exercise 20. (*) Let \(X=\{(x,y)\in \R ^2: 0 \leq y\leq x^2\}\). Prove that \(\pi _1\big (X,(0,0)\big )\) is trivial.
    Hint: Find a deformation retraction from \(X\) onto \(\R \times \{0\}\), a convex set, and apply Theorem 5.35, together with the previous exercise.

    Solution: The function \(H\colon X \times [0,1] \to X\) such that

    \[(x,y,s)\mapsto (x,y(1-s))\]

    is continuous, for each component is a polynomial in at most two variables. Clearly \(H(x,y,0)=(x,y)\) and \(H(x,y,1)=(x,0)\in \R \times \{0\}\), for each \((x,y)\in X\). Moreover every \((x,y)\in \R \times \{0\}\) must satisfy \(y=0\) and so \(H(x,y,t)=H(x,0,t)=(x,0)=(x,y)\) for each \(t \in [0,1]\). So \(\R \times \{0\}\) is a strong deformation retract of \(X\). Since \(\R \times \{0\}\) is convex, \(\pi _1\big (\R \times \{0\},(0,0)\big )\) is trivial. The previous exercise hence gives that \(\pi _1\big (X,(0,0)\big )\) is trivial. 

    ∎

  • Exercise 21. Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be topological spaces. Let \(x \in X\) and \(y \in Y\). Let \(f\colon X\rightarrow Y\) be a continuous function with \(f(x)=y\).

    Suppose that \(\pi _1(X,x)\) is infinite, whereas \(\pi _1(Y,f(x))\) is trivial. Prove that there does not exists a continuous map \(g\colon Y \rightarrow X\) with \(g(y)=x\) and such that \((g \circ f)_* : \pi _1(X,x) \rightarrow \pi _1(X,x)\) is injective.

    Solution (given): Suppose we have a continuous function \(f\colon X\to Y\) and points \(x\in X\) and \(y\in Y\) as described. Suppose we are given a continuous function \(g\colon Y \to X\) with \(g(y)=x\). We then have the following commutative diagram of pointed spaces and pointed maps,

    \[\xymatrix { & (X,x)\ar @/^2pc/[rr]^{g\circ f} \ar [r]_{f} &(Y,y) \ar [r]_{g}& (X,x)}.\]

    (The diagram being commutative means exactly that ‘going’ from \(X \to X\), directly, via \(g\circ f\) is the same as going from \(X\) to \(Y\), via \(f\), and then going from \(Y\) to \(X\), via \(g\).)

    This gives the following commutative diagram of groups, and homomorphisms between groups:

    \[\xymatrix { & \pi _1(X,y)\ar @/^2pc/[rr]^{(g\circ f)_*} \ar [r]_{f_*} &\pi _1(Y,y) \ar [r]_{g_*}& \pi _1(X,x)}.\]

    Suppose that \(\pi _1(Y,y)\) is trivial. So \(\pi (Y,y)=(\{e\},\cdot )\). Given any \(k\in \pi _1(X,x)\) we have that \((g\circ f)_*(k)=(g_*\circ f_*)(k)=g_*(e)=e_{\pi _1(X,x)}\), the identity of \(\pi _1(X,x)\). So all elements \(k \in \pi _1(X,x)\) are sent to the identity element of \(\pi _1(X,x)\), via \((g\circ f)_*\). And since \(\pi _1(X,x)\) is infinite, it cannot then be that \((g\circ f)_*\) is injective.  

    ∎

  • Exercise 22. (*) Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be topological spaces. Let \(f\colon X \to Y\) be a continuous function. Let \(x\in X\) and let \(y = f(x)\). Prove that if \(\pi _1(X,x)\) is not the trivial group, but \(\pi _1(Y,y)\) is trivial, then there does not exist a continuous map \(g\colon Y\to X\) such that \(g\circ f=\mathrm {id}_X\).

    Solution: The solution is essentially a repetition of the solution of Exercise 21. Suppose we have a function \(f\colon X\to Y\). Let \(x \in X\), fixed, and let \(y=f(x)\). Suppose we are given a continuous function \(g\colon Y \to X\) with \(g(y)=x\). We then have the following commutative diagram of pointed spaces and pointed maps.

    \[\xymatrix { & (X,x)\ar @/^2pc/[rr]^{g\circ f} \ar [r]_{f} &(Y,y) \ar [r]_{g}& (X,x)}.\]

    (The diagram being commutative means exactly that ‘going’ from \(X \to X\), directly, via \(g\circ f\) is the same as going from \(X\) to \(Y\), via \(f\), and then going from \(Y\) to \(X\), via \(g\).)

    This gives the following commutative diagram of groups, and homomorphisms between groups:

    \[\xymatrix { & \pi _1(X,x)\ar @/^2pc/[rr]^{(g\circ f)_*} \ar [r]_{f_*} &\pi _1(Y,y) \ar [r]_{g_*}& \pi _1(X,x)}.\]

    Suppose that \(\pi _1(Y,y)\) is trivial. So \(\pi (Y,y)=(\{e\},\cdot )\). Given any \(k\in \pi _1(X,x)\) we have that \((g\circ f)_*(k)=(g_*\circ f_*)(k)=g_*(e)=e_{\pi _1(X,x)}\), the identity of \(\pi _1(X,x)\). So all elements \(k \in \pi _1(X,x)\) are sent to the identity element of \(\pi _1(X,x)\), via \((g\circ f)_*\). And since \(\pi _1(X,x)\) has more than one element, it cannot then be that \((g\circ f)_*\) is injective.

    Suppose we had a function \(g\colon Y \to X\) with \(g\circ f=\id _X\). Then, as maps \(\pi _1(X,x)\to \pi _1(X,x)\), we would have \((g\circ f)_*=(\id _X)_*=\id _{\pi _1(X,x)}\). But then it would follow that \((g\circ f)_*\) would be injective; a contradiction. 

    ∎

  • Exercise 23. Construct a path-homotopy between the path \(\gamma \colon [0,1]\to \R ^2\) such that \(\gamma (t)=(t,0)\) and the path \(\gamma '\colon [0,1]\to \R ^2\) such that \(\gamma '(t)=(t^2,t-t^2)\).
    Hint Adapt the proof of Proposition 5.33. (The argument is a lot shorter as you can have an explicit form for a path-homotopy. Use the fact that polynomial functions in several variables are continuous.)

    Solution: Note that \(\gamma (0)=\gamma '(0)=(0,0)\) and \(\gamma (1)=\gamma '(1)=(1,0)\). So \(\gamma \) and \(\gamma '\) have the same initial point and the same end-point. We take \(H\colon [0,1]\times [0,1]\to \R ^2\) to be such that \(H(t,s)=(1-s)\gamma (t)+s\gamma '(t)\). So

    \[H(t,s)=\big ( (1-s)t+s t^2, s(t-t^2) \big ) .\]

    In particular, clearly:

    • • \(H(t,0)=\gamma (t)\), for all \(t \in [0,1]\),

    • • \(H(t,1)=\gamma '(t)\), for all \(t \in [0,1]\),

    • • \(H(0,s)=(0,0)\), for all \(s \in [0,1]\),

    • • \(H(1,s)=(1,0)\), for all \(s \in [0,1]\).

    Finally, \(H\colon [0,1]\times [0,1]\to \) is continuous given that its composition with the two projections \(\R ^2\to \R \), namely the component functions \((t,s)\mapsto (1-s)t+s t^2\) and \((t,s)\mapsto s(t-t^2)\), are continuous functions \([0,1]^2 \to \R \), because they are the restriction of polynomials of two variable (always continuous functions) to \([0,1]\times [0,1]\).
    NB: this solution uses again that the topology of \(\R ^2\), with the standard topology, coincides with the product topology in \(\R \times \R \), where \(\R \) is given the Euclidean topology.
